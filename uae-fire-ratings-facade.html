Getting façade fire compliance wrong in the UAE is not a minor issue — it can halt a project, trigger DCD rejection, or create serious liability. This guide tells you exactly which materials pass, which fail, and which are conditional.

⚠ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER

Fire compliance requirements in the UAE are subject to change and vary by emirate, building type, and specific project authority. Always verify current requirements directly with the relevant authority (DCD, ADCD, Trakhees, etc.) before finalising specifications. This guide is for general information only and does not constitute professional fire engineering advice.

01 / The Regulatory Framework

Façade fire compliance in the UAE is governed by a layered set of regulations that architects must navigate simultaneously:

UAE Fire and Life Safety Code of Practice 2017

The national code, published by the Ministry of Interior, sets baseline requirements for all building types across all emirates. For external cladding, it references EN 13501-1 as the fire classification standard and specifies minimum performance thresholds by building height and occupancy type.

Dubai Civil Defence (DCD)

DCD implements and enforces the national code in Dubai with additional local requirements. Following the Grenfell Tower fire in 2017, DCD issued specific circulars tightening cladding requirements for buildings above 15 metres. DCD requires cladding material approval and holds its own list of tested and approved products.

Abu Dhabi Civil Defence (ADCD)

ADCD operates similarly to DCD but with Abu Dhabi-specific requirements. Projects in Abu Dhabi, Al Ain, and Al Dhafra region must comply with ADCD requirements, which largely mirror the national code but with local enforcement nuances.

Trakhees

Projects in JAFZA, Palm Jumeirah, and other Nakheel/DP World jurisdictions fall under Trakhees authority. Trakhees maintains its own approval process for façade systems and may impose requirements beyond the base DCD requirements.

02 / Fire Classification System (EN 13501-1)

The UAE uses the European classification system EN 13501-1 for reaction to fire performance. Understanding this system is essential for reading product data sheets and compliance certificates.

03 / Height-Based Requirements

The UAE Fire Code 2017 and DCD requirements apply different cladding restrictions based on building height. These thresholds are the most critical factor in material selection for multi-storey projects.

⚠ ACM WARNING — CRITICAL FOR UAE PROJECTS

Standard Aluminium Composite Material (ACM) with a polyethylene core is banned for use on buildings above 15 metres in Dubai and across the UAE. This material, similar to what was used on Grenfell Tower, has been prohibited by DCD. Only ACM-FR (fire-resistant core, Class A2) is permitted. When specifying ACM, always confirm the core specification with the manufacturer and obtain the original EN 13501-1 test certificate. Do not accept photocopies or documents from third parties.

05 / Getting DCD Approval

For buildings requiring DCD approval of the cladding system, architects and contractors should follow this process:

DCD APPROVAL PROCESS

1.  Obtain EN 13501-1 fire test certificate from an accredited laboratory for the specific product
2.  Confirm the test covers the thickness, substrate, and fixing system as installed
3.  Submit product data sheet, test certificate, and system drawing to DCD via the Basher portal
4.  DCD reviews and issues approval letter — typically 2–4 weeks
5.  Include approval letter in the building permit application package
6.  Retain original certificates on site for inspection during construction

06 / The Whole System Rule

ARCHITECTS OFTEN MISS THIS

DCD and the UAE Fire Code assess the entire cladding system, not just the face panel. This means the fire performance of cavity insulation, fixings, adhesives, backing boards, sealants, and membranes must all be considered as part of the system. A non-combustible GFRC panel installed over combustible insulation does not constitute a non-combustible cladding system. Always specify the complete system and obtain a system-level fire test or assessment.
