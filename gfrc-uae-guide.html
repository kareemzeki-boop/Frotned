Section 01

Executive Summary

Glass Fibre Reinforced Concrete (GFRC) is a cement-based composite material produced by dispersing alkali-resistant (AR) glass fibres throughout a Portland cement matrix. In facade applications, GFRC is formed into thin-shell cladding panels typically 10–25 mm thick, offering the appearance and durability of mass concrete at a fraction of the weight. The material is classified EN 13501-1 Class A1 — non-combustible — and is therefore compliant with Dubai Civil Defence (DCD) and Abu Dhabi Civil Defence (ADCD) requirements for external cladding on buildings of all heights.

GFRC has a well-established presence in the UAE and GCC construction market. Facade elements on major institutional, hospitality, residential, and commercial projects across Dubai and Abu Dhabi have been fabricated in GFRC for three decades, supported by a network of local and regional precast manufacturers. The material’s capacity to replicate stone, travertine, and traditional Arabic architectural motifs at controlled cost and weight has made it a preferred choice for mid-range to premium facades.

This document provides the engineering reference data required to specify GFRC facade panels accurately and assess their suitability for specific project conditions in the UAE and GCC region.

Section 02

Material Overview

Composition

GFRC is composed of a Portland cement and fine aggregate matrix reinforced with alkali-resistant glass fibres at a fibre content of 3–5% by weight. The matrix typically incorporates a polymer modifier — commonly an acrylic copolymer emulsion — at 5–10% of cementitious content, which improves tensile strain capacity, reduces water permeability, and enhances impact resistance. Supplementary cementitious materials (SCM) including GGBS (ground granulated blast-furnace slag) and fly ash are frequently incorporated to reduce embodied carbon and improve workability.

The alkali-resistant glass fibre is the critical reinforcing element. Standard E-glass fibres are rapidly degraded by the high-pH environment of Portland cement; AR glass fibres with a minimum zirconia content of 16% (per EN 15191) are mandatory for long-term durability. Fibre length is typically 25–38 mm for spray-up manufacture, or longer for premix methods. Fibre orientation in spray-up GFRC is quasi-random in the plane of the panel face, producing broadly isotropic in-plane mechanical properties.

Manufacturing Methods

Three principal manufacturing processes are used for GFRC facade panels in the GCC:

  • Spray-up: A slurry of cement, sand, water, and polymer is simultaneously sprayed with chopped AR glass fibres onto a mould surface. The most widely used method for complex three-dimensional panel forms. Fibre content of 4–5% by weight is typical.
  • Premix: AR glass fibres (typically 12–25 mm length) are blended into the cementitious matrix before casting. Suitable for simpler panel geometries; fibre content limited to 3–4% due to workability constraints. Slightly lower tensile and flexural strength than spray-up panels of equivalent thickness.
  • Hybrid / face-coat system: A thin architectural face coat (6–8 mm) of spray-up GFRC is applied to the mould for surface finish quality, backed by a structural premix or spray-up layer. Common for large-format decorative panels.

Surface Finishes

GFRC accepts a wide range of surface finishes including acid-etched aggregate exposure, sandblasted texture, smooth off-mould finish, applied paint coatings, and integral pigmentation. These allow the material to replicate limestone, travertine, granite, and other natural stones with high fidelity, or to achieve contemporary smooth panel finishes for modern architectural expressions.

Section 04

Thermal Performance

GFRC facade panels function as the external weathering leaf in a rainscreen or face-fixed system. Their thermal contribution to the overall wall assembly is limited by the thinness of the panel; thermal performance of the building envelope is governed primarily by the insulation system positioned behind the cladding layer.

The relatively low coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) of GFRC (10–13 × 10&sup6; /°C) compared to aluminium (23 × 10&sup6; /°C) reduces thermal movement in large-format panels. A 2,000 mm GFRC panel subjected to a surface temperature differential of 55°C (typical for GCC summer conditions) undergoes approximately 1.1–1.4 mm of thermal expansion — roughly half the movement of an equivalent aluminium panel. Joint widths and fixing tolerances should still be designed to accommodate this movement with a safety margin.

GCC Design Note

GFRC panels in the GCC climate can reach surface temperatures of 65–75°C in direct summer sun on south and west-facing elevations. Darker integral pigments absorb significantly more solar radiation than light finishes, increasing panel surface temperature and thermal movement. Solar Reflectance Index (SRI) data for the proposed finish should be assessed during facade design, particularly for large panels with restricted movement joints.

Section 05

Fire Safety & Regulatory Compliance

GFRC is classified EN 13501-1 Class A1 — non-combustible — by virtue of its cementitious inorganic composition. This is the highest achievable fire reaction classification and renders GFRC compliant with UAE Fire and Life Safety Code 2017 requirements for external cladding on all building heights, including supertall structures.

For projects requiring formal DCD Technical Approval submission, the GFRC panel manufacturer should provide the following documentation: EN 13501-1 classification test certificate from a UKAS or equivalent accredited laboratory, material data sheet confirming AR glass fibre content and zirconia specification, and an independent test certificate for the proposed surface coating if an applied coating is specified (coatings can affect the composite assembly’s fire classification).

Compliance Note — Applied Coatings

GFRC panels with an applied organic coating (paint or polymer-based finish) must carry a composite fire test certificate that includes the coating layer. An A1 classification for the bare GFRC substrate does not automatically extend to the finished panel if an organic coating of significant thickness is applied. For water-based acrylic coatings below 200 microns total dry film thickness, the A1 classification is generally maintained; this should be confirmed by the coating manufacturer with reference to EN 13501-1 test data.

Section 06

Structural & Installation Considerations

Panel Sizing and Weight

GFRC facade panels are typically produced in maximum sizes of 6 m × 2 m for flat panels, with practical maximum dimensions for complex three-dimensional forms governed by mould size and site crane capacity. Panel weight of 30–55 kg/m² is substantially heavier than aluminium or GFRP cladding but significantly lighter than conventional precast concrete (typically 240–300 kg/m² at 100 mm thickness), making GFRC practical in situations where heavy precast is structurally or logistically problematic.

Fixing Systems

GFRC panels are attached to the building structure by one of three primary systems:

  • Steel stud frame system: Welded steel stud frames are bonded into the back of the GFRC panel during manufacture. The frame provides the primary structural support and transfers loads to the building facade bracket system. Standard for large or structurally complex panels.
  • Mechanical cast-in anchor system: Stainless steel anchors cast into the panel body transfer load to the support structure through adjustable bracket connections. Requires precise panel manufacturing tolerance and careful structural design of the anchor zone.
  • Face-fixed system: Used for smaller panels. Stainless steel screws or bolts pass through the panel into framing behind. Less preferred for architectural facades due to visible fixings, though countersunk and plugged details are available.

All fixings and embedded components in contact with GFRC must be 316-grade stainless steel as a minimum. Carbon steel and standard 304-grade stainless steel are susceptible to corrosion in the alkaline GFRC matrix and in coastal GCC environments; their use is not recommended.

Tolerances and Jointing

GFRC panels are manufactured to tighter dimensional tolerances than conventional precast concrete, typically ±3 mm on face dimensions and ±2 mm on thickness for flat panels. Joint widths of 10–20 mm are standard, accommodating manufacturing tolerance, installation tolerance, and thermal movement. Open-drained rainscreen joints are the preferred detail for primary facades; silicone-sealed joints are used where a watertight finish is required at recessed or horizontal joint conditions.

Structural Design

Panel structural design should follow the GRCA International Design Guide or the PCI Architectural Precast Concrete Manual as applicable. Wind load design should reference ASCE 7 or BS EN 1991-1-4 with UAE National Annex values. DCD requires third-party structural review of facade panel connections for buildings above 10 storeys.

Section 07

Durability in GCC Climate

GFRC has demonstrated acceptable long-term durability in GCC conditions when correctly specified, manufactured, and maintained. The principal durability risks specific to the region are UV-induced surface degradation of applied coatings, alkali-silica reaction (ASR) in poorly specified mixes, long-term AR glass fibre strength retention, and water absorption effects in the context of high thermal cycling.

The long-term mechanical performance of GFRC is influenced by progressive changes in the glass fibre-cement interface over time. Accelerated ageing tests to EN 1169 (hot water immersion method) are used to predict 25-year in-service mechanical properties. GRCA member manufacturers are required to conduct this testing and report aged MOR and PEL values. Specifiers should request aged property data and confirm that the design load cases are satisfied using aged, not fresh, material properties.

Section 09

Material Selection Guidance

GFRC is an appropriate facade material selection for UAE and GCC projects meeting the following conditions. These criteria should be assessed by the facade consultant in the context of the specific project brief and regulatory environment.

GFRC is technically appropriate where:

  • The project requires a non-combustible cladding material (EN 13501-1 Class A1) for any building height — GFRC satisfies DCD requirements without restriction
  • Complex three-dimensional facade geometry, traditional Arabic motifs, or stone-replication finishes are required at a cost below natural stone or precast concrete
  • Building structure or support brackets have limited capacity for heavy cladding dead loads — GFRC is substantially lighter than natural stone or conventional precast concrete while maintaining non-combustible status
  • A design service life of 40–60 years is targeted, with a conventional maintenance programme
  • Local UAE manufacture is preferred to reduce supply chain risk and lead time

Specification requirements for UAE projects:

  • AR glass fibre to EN 15191, minimum 16% ZrO² content; fibre content 4–5% by weight for spray-up panels
  • Polymer-modified matrix with acrylic emulsion at 5–10% of cementitious content
  • Aged mechanical properties (EN 1169 hot water ageing) provided by manufacturer, confirming design PEL and MOR values
  • All embedded fixings and anchors: AISI 316 stainless steel minimum
  • EN 13501-1 Class A1 classification certificate from UKAS-accredited laboratory
  • GRCA membership or equivalent quality management certification from the manufacturer
  • Water absorption test to EN 13295; maximum 12% by mass for UAE coastal applications
Procurement Note

GFRC facade panels for significant UAE projects should be sourced from manufacturers with demonstrated experience in the GCC climate, either locally or from established regional suppliers. The panel manufacturer should be involved in the design development phase to confirm fixing system geometry, tolerances, and the structural design of the panel-to-structure connection. Late design changes to panel geometry or fixing positions are costly and can compromise structural adequacy.
