Section 01

Executive Summary

GFRP and aluminium represent two technically distinct facade cladding strategies with different regulatory positions, durability profiles, and cost implications for GCC construction. Aluminium — in solid sheet, extruded cassette, or aluminium composite material (ACM) with a verified fire-retardant mineral core — is a well-established non-combustible cladding option appropriate for buildings of all heights under the UAE Fire and Life Safety Code 2017.

GFRP offers material advantages in structural weight, design geometry freedom, and thermal insulation performance. However, its combustibility classification — typically EN 13501-1 Class B or C — restricts application to buildings below 23 metres under current Dubai Civil Defence (DCD) circulars. For projects within this height limit, GFRP presents a technically viable and frequently cost-competitive alternative.

Material selection should be driven by building height and fire strategy, programme constraints, design geometry complexity, durability requirements, and lifecycle cost targets. This document provides the technical reference data to support a structured comparative analysis.

Section 02

Material Overview

Glass Fibre Reinforced Polymer (GFRP)

GFRP — also referred to as Glass Reinforced Plastic (GRP) — is a composite material comprising a polymer resin matrix (polyester, vinyl ester, or epoxy) reinforced with glass fibre in woven, chopped-strand mat, or unidirectional orientations. Facade panels are typically manufactured by hand lay-up, resin transfer moulding (RTM), or vacuum infusion, yielding flat or profiled elements with an integral gel coat or applied coating finish.

Mechanical and thermal properties of GFRP are anisotropic and dependent on fibre volume fraction (typically 30–40% for facade-grade panels), fibre orientation, and resin system. Published values throughout this document reflect these typical parameters.

Aluminium

Aluminium facade cladding encompasses solid rolled sheet (typically 2–4 mm), extruded profiles, and aluminium composite material (ACM). Standard alloy grades for cladding panels are 5005 and 5052 for sheet applications, and 6063-T5 or 6061-T6 for extruded framing and cassette systems. Surface finish is typically PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride) coating to AAMA 2605 for high-durability applications, or polyester powder coating for lower-exposure environments.

Regulatory Note — ACM Panels

Aluminium Composite Material with polyethylene (PE) cores is prohibited in the UAE for external cladding following DCD Circular 2/2020. Only ACM with a verified fire-retardant mineral core achieving EN 13501-1 Class A2 or better is permitted on any building in the UAE. Independent test certification is required — supplier declarations are not accepted as DCD compliance evidence.

Section 04

Thermal Performance

Facade thermal performance is a primary design parameter in the GCC climate, where sustained ambient temperatures of 40–50°C and global horizontal irradiance commonly exceeding 2,200 kWh/m²/year impose significant cooling loads on building envelopes. The thermal properties of the cladding panel material influence HVAC load calculations, wall U-value, and dimensional movement under solar gain.

The thermal conductivity of GFRP (0.25–0.40 W/m·K) is 400–600 times lower than aluminium (160–200 W/m·K). In a drained and ventilated rainscreen system, both materials function as external cladding leaves with minimal insulation contribution; wall thermal performance is governed primarily by the insulation layer behind. However, GFRP generates fewer thermal bridging paths at fixings and brackets compared to aluminium systems where metal-to-metal contact at fixing points creates preferential heat flow paths through the insulation zone.

GCC Thermal Movement Note

Panel surface temperatures in direct summer sun in Dubai can reach 70–80°C. At a panel temperature differential of 55°C (from pre-dawn minimum to peak solar gain), a 1,500 mm aluminium panel undergoes approximately 1.9 mm of thermal expansion. A GFRP panel of equivalent length undergoes 1.5–2.1 mm. Both require designed movement joints of 8–12 mm minimum at panel perimeters, and slotted or floating fixing details to prevent restraint cracking at fixing points.

Section 05

Fire Safety & Regulatory Compliance

Fire safety classification is the decisive regulatory differentiator between GFRP and aluminium in the UAE. The UAE Fire and Life Safety Code 2017, enforced by Dubai Civil Defence (DCD) and Abu Dhabi Civil Defence (ADCD), specifies minimum EN 13501-1 fire reaction classifications for external cladding materials based on building height.

GFRP Fire Performance

Standard GFRP panels with polyester or vinyl ester resin achieve EN 13501-1 Class B or C under small flame (ISO 11925-2) and single burning item (SBI, EN 13823) testing. Fire-retardant resin systems can achieve Class B-s2, d0 but cannot achieve Class A1 or A2 (non-combustible), as the organic resin matrix inherently contributes to combustion. GFRP is therefore not compliant for external cladding on buildings above 23 metres under DCD Circular 2/2020.

Aluminium Fire Performance

Solid aluminium sheet and extrusions are EN 13501-1 Class A1 (non-combustible). ACM with a verified FR mineral core achieves Class A2-s1, d0. Both are compliant for all building heights. For ACM, specifiers must confirm the specific product brand and construction carry a current DCD-accepted fire test certificate. The mineral core content must be independently verified at a minimum of 70% by weight — a requirement introduced in DCD post-Grenfell circulars.

Regulatory Warning

GFRP is not permitted as primary external cladding on buildings above 23 metres in the UAE under DCD Circular 2/2020. Any proposed use of GFRP above ground floor level on buildings approaching this height threshold requires a fire engineer's report and DCD Technical Approval prior to specification.

Section 06

Structural & Installation Considerations

Panel Fixing Systems

Both materials are typically installed as drained and ventilated rainscreen systems on aluminium or hot-dip galvanised steel sub-frame structures. Fixing design differs due to the contrasting mechanical properties of the two materials.

Aluminium panels are fixed using concealed mechanical clips, face-fixed rivets or screws, or bonded cassette return systems. The ductility and predictable yield behaviour of aluminium alloy allows standard structural calculations to govern fixing design. Wind load testing to ASTM E330 is standard practice for panels exceeding 1.5 m² surface area in high-wind-zone locations.

GFRP panels require specific attention to fixing detail due to low ductility (1–2% elongation at break) and susceptibility to stress concentration at fixing holes. Oversized fixing holes with compressible washers, or slotted fixing arrangements, are necessary to accommodate thermal movement without inducing cracking at fixing points. Pull-out and shear capacity testing of the specific fixing detail in the panel substrate should be commissioned for each project.

Galvanic Compatibility

GFRP is electrochemically inert, eliminating galvanic corrosion risk between panel and aluminium sub-frame. For steel support structures in coastal GCC locations, A4-grade stainless steel fasteners are required for both material types to prevent bi-metallic corrosion.

Panel Weight and Site Logistics

At 5–6 kg/m², GFRP panels are approximately 25–35% lighter than equivalent aluminium sheet at 8 kg/m². For large panel formats exceeding 3 m², this weight difference materially simplifies on-site handling and reduces crane lift requirements. The reduced dead load also decreases bracket and sub-frame sizing, with potential structural cost savings on large-area facades.

Section 07

Durability in GCC Climate

The GCC built environment presents specific durability challenges for external cladding: sustained UV radiation intensity (UV index regularly exceeding 11), ambient temperatures of 45°C+, coastal salt-laden air in shoreline and marina developments, and abrasive wind-blown sand in inland and desert-fringe locations.

Maintenance requirementGel coat inspection every 10 years; recoating at 15–20 year intervals typicalLow — PVDF coating requires no routine maintenance; fixing and sealant inspection at 10-year intervals

UV-induced degradation of the GFRP resin matrix and gel coat causes surface chalking, progressive colour change, and — in unprotected panels — surface fibre prominence and mechanical property reduction. UV-stabilised resin systems combined with pigmented, UV-resistant gel coats extend the maintenance-free service period substantially. Specifiers should require ISO 4892-2 (Xenon arc) or ISO 4892-3 (UV fluorescent lamp) accelerated weathering test data for the specific resin-gel coat system. UV-stabilised GFRP panels with rated gel coats have demonstrated acceptable performance on low-to-medium-rise GCC projects across multiple property cycles.

Aluminium with PVDF coating to AAMA 2605 — specifying 70% PVDF resin minimum — provides a well-characterised high-durability finish with documented performance in GCC conditions. The PVDF system has an established track record across landmark projects in Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, and Doha. For projects with long design service lives, limited maintenance access, or operator preference for low intervention, aluminium presents the more predictable long-term durability profile.

On a whole-life cost basis, aluminium typically presents the lower net cost for standard rectangular or lightly profiled facades due to its longer service life, minimal maintenance requirement, and residual material value at end of life. GFRP can present a lower overall project cost where its design freedom eliminates expensive fabrication costs for complex geometry, or where weight reduction enables a lighter structural system with material savings upstream in the structural engineering.

Environmental performance assessment should consider the embodied carbon of each system across the full lifecycle, including extraction, manufacturing, installation, maintenance, and end-of-life. Aluminium production is energy-intensive; however, the high recycled content achievable (30–95% post-consumer recycled aluminium is commercially available) and indefinite recyclability provide a favourable end-of-life position. GFRP has lower embodied carbon in production but offers essentially no end-of-life value.

Section 09

Material Selection Guidance

The following criteria summarise the conditions under which each material is technically appropriate for UAE and GCC facade applications. These are engineering reference points — final material selection requires project-specific analysis by the facade consultant and, where applicable, an independent fire engineer.

GFRP is technically appropriate where:

  • Building height is confirmed below 23 metres and fire engineering confirms DCD compliance for the specific application
  • Complex curved, sculptural, or parametric panel geometry is required that would be cost-prohibitive or technically demanding in fabricated aluminium
  • Minimising facade dead load is a structural design objective — particularly relevant for refurbishment projects with constrained structural capacity
  • The project is in a highly corrosive marine or industrial environment where metallic corrosion resistance is a primary durability requirement
  • Programme constraints favour factory-moulded panels that can be manufactured to complex form without on-site fabrication

Aluminium is technically appropriate where:

  • Building height exceeds 23 metres — aluminium is the code-compliant choice for mid-rise, high-rise, and supertall buildings under UAE regulations
  • A design service life of 40+ years with a low-maintenance specification is required
  • The project specification mandates demonstrable EN 13501-1 Class A2 or A1 non-combustible cladding for any height
  • Standard flat or lightly profiled panel geometry is sufficient — aluminium provides the most cost-effective and technically established solution
  • Sustainability targets include specified recycled material content or end-of-life recyclability
Specification Note

For projects where both materials are technically feasible — low-rise buildings below 23 metres with moderate geometry complexity — the selection decision typically resolves around long-term appearance durability and maintenance strategy. Aluminium with PVDF coating provides the more predictable long-term facade appearance. GFRP specifications should include a clearly defined inspection and maintenance programme in the O&M documentation, with a budgeted gel coat inspection interval and a defined recoating trigger condition.
